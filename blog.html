Workplace Accessibility

DEI Is Not Dead. For Deaf and Disabled Employees, It Just Got More Urgent.

The headlines have been relentless. Executive orders. Federal DEI office closures. Corporate retreats from diversity programs. It can look, from a distance, like DEI is over.

It is not over. And for employees who are Deaf or disabled, the legal and moral obligations of employers have not changed by a single word.

What Has Actually Changed

Several large companies have scaled back voluntary DEI commitments in response to political pressure. Some federal agencies have paused or restructured programs. These are real changes with real consequences for certain communities.

What has not changed: the Americans with Disabilities Act. Section 501 and 503 of the Rehabilitation Act. State-level disability rights laws, many of which exceed federal protections. The legal requirement to provide reasonable accommodations to qualified employees with disabilities. The legal requirement to engage in an interactive process when accommodation is requested.

None of that was an executive order. None of it went away.

The Specific Stakes for Deaf Employees

For employees who are Deaf or hard of hearing, workplace accessibility is not a perk. It is a legal floor.

Meetings without interpreters, training sessions without captioning, performance reviews conducted without accessible communication — these are not oversights. In many cases they are ADA violations. The fact that a company has quietly removed its DEI page from its website does not change this.

The demand for qualified ASL interpreters in workplace settings has not declined. If anything, increased attention to DEI rollbacks has made Deaf employees and their advocates more vigilant about documenting failures.

What Employers in San Diego Need to Understand

California maintains some of the strongest disability rights protections in the country. The Fair Employment and Housing Act provides protections that exceed federal baselines. Employers who assume that reduced federal DEI enforcement translates to reduced California enforcement are making a costly mistake.

San Diego employers who have Deaf employees, or who recruit from the Deaf community, need reliable interpretation services for:

  • Onboarding and orientation sessions
  • Regular team meetings and all-hands
  • One-on-one performance conversations
  • Training and professional development
  • Disciplinary meetings, where the stakes of communication failure are especially high

Providing a qualified ASL interpreter is not a DEI initiative. It is a basic reasonable accommodation. The distinction matters right now.

The Opportunity in the Noise

For organizations that want to demonstrate genuine commitment to disability inclusion while others pull back, this is a moment. Quiet, consistent, legally compliant accessibility practices speak louder than any DEI page.

Medical & Healthcare

Your Deaf Patient Just Walked In. Does Your San Diego Hospital Have a Plan?

You have about sixty seconds from the moment a Deaf patient walks into your emergency department before the communication failure becomes a liability.

That is not an exaggeration. Under the Americans with Disabilities Act and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, healthcare facilities receiving federal funding are required to provide effective communication to patients with disabilities. For Deaf patients who use American Sign Language, effective communication typically means a qualified ASL interpreter. Not a family member. Not a staff member who knows a few signs. A qualified interpreter.

Most San Diego hospitals and clinics know this in theory. Fewer have an actual plan.

What the Law Requires

The ADA has been on the books since 1990, and HHS guidance is clear: healthcare providers must provide auxiliary aids and services, including qualified interpreters, when necessary for effective communication. The decision about what aid is appropriate is not left entirely to the provider. It must be based on the patient’s expressed communication preference.

Video Remote Interpreting, or VRI, is commonly used as a cost-effective alternative to in-person interpretation. When implemented correctly — with adequate screen size, stable connection, and appropriate positioning — it can be effective. When it fails, whether due to technical issues or improper setup, it is not a legal safe harbor.

Refusing to accept a patient until an interpreter is available, or delaying care in ways that compromise outcomes, can expose facilities to OCR complaints and civil litigation.

The Gap Between Policy and Practice

Many providers have an ADA coordinator. Many have a VRI cart somewhere in a supply closet. What fewer have is a reliable process for the middle of a Saturday night when the cart is not charged, the on-call manager does not know the interpreter request protocol, and a Deaf patient is waiting to be triaged.

The Joint Commission has flagged communication as a leading root cause of sentinel events. Deaf and hard-of-hearing patients face disproportionate risks in medical settings precisely because communication failures compound at every point of contact — from intake to discharge instructions.

What Good Preparation Looks Like

A well-prepared San Diego healthcare facility has pre-credentialed relationships with interpretation agencies that can provide qualified ASL interpreters on short notice, both in-person and via VRI. Staff at every level — not just admissions — understand how to initiate an interpreter request. There is a documented escalation path when primary options are unavailable.

For outpatient clinics and specialty practices, the standard is similar. Scheduling an ASL interpreter for a known Deaf patient is not above and beyond. It is the legal baseline.

Deaf Culture & History

Black Deaf History Month and the Overlooked Leaders Who Built Modern ASL

February is Black History Month. It is also, for many in the Deaf community, a time to honor a history that rarely makes it into mainstream recognition: the history of Black Deaf Americans and their profound, foundational contributions to American Sign Language and Deaf culture.

Most people who have heard of ASL know a few names. They know that Laurent Clerc, a Deaf French educator, came to America in 1817 and helped establish the first permanent school for the Deaf in Hartford, Connecticut. What is far less widely known is how Black Deaf Americans — particularly through the segregated schools of the American South — developed their own signing traditions that eventually enriched and diversified ASL itself.

The Black ASL Tradition

For roughly a century — from the post-Civil War era through desegregation in the 1960s — Black Deaf students attended separate schools. This was not simply a tragedy of exclusion. It was also a crucible. In those schools, Black Deaf children developed a distinct signing variety, now studied as Black ASL, with its own vocabulary, grammatical patterns, and cultural expressions.

Researchers including Dr. Ceil Lucas and Dr. Carolyn McCaskill have documented how Black ASL preserves older forms of ASL signs that have since changed in mainstream white Deaf communities. In some ways, Black ASL is a living archive of the language’s history.

Leaders Worth Knowing

Andrew Foster is among the most celebrated Black Deaf educators in American history. A native of Alabama who became the first Black Deaf graduate of Gallaudet University in 1954, Foster went on to found more than 30 schools for the Deaf across Africa. His work brought ASL-based education to tens of thousands of Deaf Africans.

Junius Wilson spent 76 years institutionalized in a North Carolina psychiatric facility, largely due to a wrongful commitment tied to racial injustice and his Deafness. His story, finally brought to public attention in the 1990s, illustrates the compounded vulnerabilities faced by Black Deaf individuals in a system that failed them on multiple fronts.

Nansea Higgins, Shirley Childress, and other Black Deaf women activists organized within both Deaf rights and civil rights movements, often working in spaces where they were invisible to both communities.

Why This Matters for Interpreters and the Industry

Understanding Black Deaf history is not just a matter of historical interest. It has practical implications for anyone working in ASL interpretation.

Black ASL is a distinct variety. Interpreters who are only trained in mainstream white ASL may misread or inadequately serve Black Deaf clients. Cultural competency in this community requires awareness of different signing conventions, community norms, and historical context.

The ASL interpretation field has historically been predominantly white. Organizations including the Registry of Interpreters for the Deaf have actively worked to address this, but the gap remains. For agencies like Rose Sign Language Interpreting, this means actively seeking interpreters who have genuine fluency in Black ASL and deep cultural relationships with Black Deaf communities in San Diego and beyond.

Honoring this history is not a February-only obligation. It is a year-round professional standard.

Technology & ASL

AI Sign Language Interpreters Are Going Viral. Here’s What Deaf People Actually Think.

Every few months, a new video goes viral. A tech demo shows an AI avatar signing in American Sign Language. The caption reads something like “The future of deaf communication is here.” The comments fill with hearing people calling it revolutionary. And in Deaf community forums, the reaction is often very different.

That gap tells you almost everything you need to know about where AI sign language technology actually stands.

What the Technology Does Well

Let us be clear: AI-powered sign language recognition and generation is a genuine engineering achievement. Companies like SignAll, MotionSavvy, and various university research groups have made real progress. Some systems can recognize common ASL signs with reasonable accuracy in controlled environments. Some avatar systems can generate basic signed content from text.

For certain limited applications, these tools have value. Automated closed captions in video tools. Early-stage educational apps for people learning ASL. Assistive keyboards that let Deaf users type faster using sign recognition.

These are legitimate contributions.

What It Cannot Do

What AI cannot do, in 2026, is replace a qualified human ASL interpreter in any context that matters.

ASL is not English in sign form. It is a complete, independent language with its own grammar, syntax, spatial grammar, non-manual markers (facial expressions that carry grammatical meaning), and regional variation. A system trained primarily on data from one region will misread signs common in another. A system that does not model facial grammar will miss questions, conditionals, negations, and emotional nuance.

More importantly, interpretation is not translation. A skilled interpreter does not just convert words between languages. They navigate register, culture, intent, and context in real time. In a medical appointment, they catch a patient’s hesitation and know when to slow down. In a courtroom, they render legalese in a way that preserves meaning without distorting it. In a mental health session, they hold space for fragile communication without interrupting flow.

No AI system can do that today. Not reliably. Not ethically.

What the Deaf Community Is Actually Saying

The frustration you see in Deaf community discussions about AI interpreting demos is not technophobia. It is the frustration of a community that has watched the hearing world repeatedly announce solutions to problems it does not fully understand.

The problem is not that there are not enough signs being produced in the room. The problem is that Deaf people are routinely denied access to qualified human interpreters, treated as afterthoughts in medical and legal systems, and asked to accept lower-quality accommodations.

An AI avatar that signs at a basic level does not solve that. It potentially makes it worse by giving institutions a cheap excuse to avoid providing what the law actually requires: effective communication.

Some Deaf advocates have been direct: they want technology that supports interpreters and improves access, not technology marketed as a replacement for the real thing before it is remotely ready to be.

The Real Opportunity for Technology

There is a version of AI that genuinely helps. Better interpreter scheduling platforms. Tools that help Deaf individuals find qualified interpreters in their area. Caption technology that improves access in environments where live interpreters are not feasible. Training tools that help interpreters develop skills.

ADA Compliance

ADA at 35: Is Your San Diego Business Actually Compliant — or Just Hoping Nobody Notices?

The Americans with Disabilities Act turned 35 this year. Three and a half decades since President George H.W. Bush signed the most sweeping disability civil rights law in American history — and San Diego businesses are still getting it wrong.

Not intentionally. Not maliciously. Just quietly, through omission. The unscheduled meeting with a Deaf employee, the hospital intake form with no interpreter request process, the legal deposition that moves forward without qualified ASL interpretation.

ADA compliance is not a checkbox. It is an ongoing obligation. For Deaf and hard-of-hearing individuals in San Diego County — home to more than 50,000 people with significant hearing loss — the gap between what the law requires and what businesses actually provide is still enormous.

What Does the ADA Actually Require?

Under Title III of the ADA, places of public accommodation must provide effective communication to people with disabilities. For Deaf individuals, that often means a qualified sign language interpreter.

The key word is qualified. A bilingual employee who knows some signs, a family member, or a video relay service on a smartphone does not meet the legal standard in high-stakes situations. Medical appointments, legal proceedings, mental health services, and employment meetings all typically require a certified ASL interpreter.

Title II extends these requirements to state and local government agencies: courts, schools, DMV offices, public health departments, transit agencies. If your San Diego city department or county office does not have an interpreter request protocol, you are already behind.

The Costly Mistake Most Businesses Do Not See Coming

Many San Diego businesses assume they are compliant because they have never been sued. That is not compliance. That is luck.

ADA complaints can be filed with the Department of Justice, the California Civil Rights Department, or through private litigation. California Unruh Civil Rights Act adds a private right of action with minimum statutory damages of $4,000 per violation. California courts have ruled that each instance of denied access is a separate violation.

The healthcare sector has been particularly scrutinized. Under Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act, any healthcare entity receiving federal funds must provide qualified interpreters for Deaf patients. Sharp HealthCare, Scripps Health, and Rady Children’s Hospital have robust programs. Smaller clinics, urgent care centers, and specialty practices often fall dangerously short.

San Diego’s Unique Compliance Landscape

San Diego has a thriving Deaf community and a military population that includes veterans with service-related hearing loss. The regional demand for ASL interpretation services is significant and growing.

California has stricter disability rights laws than federal minimums, so San Diego businesses face one of the highest compliance bars in the country.

What Proactive Compliance Looks Like

The businesses that get this right do not wait for a complaint. They:

  • Build an interpreter request workflow into their client intake process
  • Know the difference between VRI and in-person interpretation and understand when each is appropriate
  • Maintain relationships with local ASL interpreting agencies for on-call scheduling
  • Train front desk and HR staff to recognize interpreter requests and respond properly
  • Document every accommodation request and how it was fulfilled

The difference between a business that faces a fine and one that does not often comes down to one thing: having a plan before the Deaf patient, client, or employee walks through the door.
